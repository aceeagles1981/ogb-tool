Project Cargo & DSU Underwriting

      
PCI/PCDSU interactive guide — critical items, shipping schedules, DSU basis, survey warranty and pricing.

    

    

      
      
    

  


  
  

    
    
    
    
    
    
  


  
  

    

      

        
What is Project Cargo?

        

          Large-scale, one-off shipments of capital equipment required to build or commission an industrial project — power plants, LNG terminals, offshore platforms, wind farms, mining facilities, data centres. Items are typically oversized (OOG), high-value, long-lead, and critical path: if one piece is lost or damaged, the entire project timeline shifts.


          Key industries: Oil & gas, wind/solar energy, petrochemicals, mining, nuclear, modular construction, telecoms infrastructure.
        

      

      

        
Why DSU Exists

        

          A standard cargo policy pays for the physical loss — replacement cost of a damaged transformer. But if the transformer takes 18 months to manufacture and ship, the project sits idle. DSU covers the consequential financial loss during that delay — lost revenue, continuing debt service, fixed costs.


          This is one of the very few situations in marine insurance where a cargo owner receives consequential loss cover. It is always written alongside the cargo policy — never standalone.
        

      

    


    

      
The Two-Policy Gap — why clients need both sections

      

        If critical equipment is damaged in transit, the construction/erection (CAR/EAR) policy won't cover it — the damage happened at sea. The cargo policy pays physical loss but not delay. The DSU policy fills the gap. Without PCI+DSU, a project owner sitting on a delayed LNG plant has no cover for the revenue loss while their turbine is being remanufactured. Lenders typically require DSU as a condition of project finance.
      

    


    

      

        
SECTION 1 — Project Cargo (PCI)

        
Physical loss/damage to materials, equipment and supplies in transit. Essentially a bespoke ICC(A)/War/Strikes placement. Critical items require survey warranty. Standard cargo exclusions apply.

      

      

        
SECTION 2 — Delay in Start Up (DSU)

        
Consequential financial loss from delay to Scheduled Commercial Operation Date (SCOD). Triggered by a Section 1 cargo loss event, or H&M/hull breakdown, GA. Indemnity = Gross Profit loss OR Fixed Costs + Debt Service + ICW.

      

      

        
THE LINK — MD prerequisite

        
DSU is triggered by a material damage event under Section 1. No cargo claim = no DSU trigger (except H&M breakdown, GA). Section 1 must cover Marine + War + Strikes — this is a condition precedent to Section 2 recovery.

      

    

  


  
  

    
Information required at submission — tick items as confirmed

    

    

      
Notes on critical information

      

        Replacement time is the single most important DSU input. It drives the maximum theoretical delay, which in turn drives the indemnity period and sum insured. Post-COVID supply chain disruption has dramatically extended lead times for turbines, transformers and HVDC equipment — items that previously had 12-month lead times now run 24–36 months. Always verify against current manufacturer quoted lead times, not historical data.


        Shipping schedule must be confirmed before binding, not estimated. The premium for Section 1 cannot be calculated without it. For Section 2, the SCOD drives the entire exposure period — a revision to SCOD requires immediate notification to underwriters (Duty of Insured clause).
      

    

  


  
  

    

      

        
Basis of Indemnity (pick one at inception)

        

          
Option A — Gross Profit
Rate of GP × shortfall in Turnover during Indemnity Period. Mirrors business interruption methodology. Appropriate where the project generates measurable revenue once operational.

          
Option B — Fixed Costs + Debt Service
Ongoing fixed costs and debt servicing payments that continue regardless of production. Common for project-financed assets where lender protection is the primary driver. Risk: can over-indemnify if the project would have been loss-making anyway — underwriters must model projected revenues.

          
Option C — ICW (Increased Cost of Working)
Always added alongside A or B. Pays additional expenditure to avoid or minimise delay — e.g. air-freighting replacement parts, parallel manufacturing, temporary hire.

        

      

      

        

          
Time Deductible (waiting period)

          
Expressed as aggregate days of indemnifiable delay deducted from any claim. Market best practice: 30 days minimum, 45–60 days preferred. The deductible is proportional — it is not simply days at the beginning but is calculated as (total indemnity ÷ actual delay days) × deductible days. An aggregate deductible means delays from multiple events accumulate before the policy pays.

        

        

          
Indemnity Period

          
Starts on the planned completion date (had the event not occurred). Ends on actual completion of works including full operational testing. Capped at the policy maximum. Typical project duration: 12–36 months. DSU indemnity period generally should not exceed 12 months. Revision to SCOD requires prompt notification — underwriters may review rates on receipt.

        

        

          
⚠ Final Positioning Exclusion

          
Once a critical item begins installation directly from the carrying conveyance at site, DSU cover stops. A transformer being lifted off a barge directly onto its foundation: if it drops at that moment, it is a cargo loss but no DSU. Plan for temporary storage/laydown to maintain DSU coverage through the installation phase.

        

      

    


    

      
DSU Triggers (what activates Section 2)

      

        
1.1 Cargo loss
Loss/damage/delay to Project Cargo that triggers (or would trigger but for deductible) a Section 1 claim.

        
1.2 H&M breakdown
Loss/damage/mechanical breakdown of the carrying vessel, craft or aircraft hull or machinery. Covered per CL285/CL295/AVN16.

        
1.3 Land vehicle
Loss or mechanical breakdown of motor or rail vehicle carrying Project Cargo.

        
1.4 GA / Salvage
Vessel/conveyance involved in General Average, salvage or life saving operation.

      

    


    

      
Key Exclusions from DSU (Section 2)

      

        Fines, penalties, LDs and punitive damages (unless specifically included) • Alterations/improvements after damage • Import licence lapse (unless caused by insured peril) • Government commandeering/requisition (unless covered under CL295) • Final Positioning • Contractor's own materials (not Project Cargo) • Public authority restrictions • Non-availability of funds (this is a frequent client misconception — cash flow problems are not covered)
      

    

  


  
  

    

      
The Marine Warranty Surveyor (MWS)

      

        Project cargo insurance is essentially unwritable without a named Marine Warranty Surveyor for Critical Items. The MWS role spans the entire transit — this is "cradle to grave" risk management, not a checkbox. No reputable Lloyd's underwriter will entertain project cargo without one.
        


        What the MWS does:
        
    
          
  • Pre-shipment inspection: packaging, lifting points, condition, marking
    • 
          
  • Reviews method statement for lifting, handling, stowage and securing
    • 
          
  • Inspects carrying vessel suitability: deck strength, securing points, crane SWL, CSS Code compliance
    • 
          
  • Attends loading and unloading — witnesses operations and enforces method statement
    • 
          
  • Approves on-deck stowage or ocean barge tow arrangements
    • 
          
  • For second-hand/non-OEM items: approves packaging
    • 
          
  • Route survey for road legs: bridge heights, road bearing capacity, police escort requirements, turning circles
    • 
        

      

    


    

      

        
72-Hour Notification Rule

        
Assured must notify the Named Surveyor (or their customary notification office) at least 72 hours before estimated load/discharge dates. If surveyor then fails to attend, this is deemed not to be a breach of the warranty. Protects the insured when the surveyor is unavailable — provided the notification was given.

      

      

        
Breach of Survey Warranty

        
Breach does not void the policy — it reduces cover. The affected shipment defaults to:
• Main shipments: ICC(B) without Clause 1.3 (i.e. no washing overboard/entry of sea water)
• On-deck/barge shipments: ICC(C) + War + Strikes
Other complying shipments remain on full policy terms. If the insured subsequently complies, full cover is reinstated — but the burden of proof for any prior loss rests with the insured.

      

    


    

      
Survey Warranty Exemptions

      
Survey warranty does not apply where:

      (1) Critical items shipped in full/sole use fully enclosed containers door-to-door, OR by airfreight (other than heavy lift aircraft), AND

      (2) The Named Surveyor deems proposed methodology acceptable and waives attendance; OR

      (3) Surveyor deems component does not necessitate survey; OR

      (4) Agreement Parties agree to waive for specific shipment.


      Remote survey (COVID amendment): JCC updated the warranty during COVID-19 to allow survey by video/Zoom where physical attendance is impossible — subject to surveyor confirmation that remote methodology is adequate. Underwriters should ensure the named surveyor has this capability for projects in restricted-access locations.
      

    

  


  
  

    

      

        
Section 1 Rating (PCI — Cargo)

        

          Premium is calculated on the total project cargo value — all items on the shipping schedule. Rate depends on:

          
    
            
  • Nature of cargo: electrical/electronic equipment commands higher rates than structural steel
    • 
            
  • Routing: high-theft corridors, transhipment frequency, port quality, road transport risk
    • 
            
  • Vessel quality: CL354 classification compliance; heavy lift vs container vs barge
    • 
            
  • Packing standard: export crating, sea-fast stowage, OOG securing plans
    • 
            
  • Storage periods: on-site laydown adds static risk; ports in flood zones
    • 
            
  • Project type: offshore/FPSO higher than onshore wind
    • 
          

          Shipping schedule must be confirmed — premium cannot be calculated on estimates.
        

      

      

        
Section 2 Rating (DSU)

        

          Two recognised rating methods:
          

            1. Daily Indemnity Rate: Premium = Daily indemnity value × Maximum indemnity days × DSU rate%. DSU rate reflects probability and duration of delay. Requires clear daily value calculation.
          

          

            2. Marine Rate: Premium calculated as a percentage of the total DSU limit (sum insured). More common where the daily indemnity is variable or the project has multiple simultaneous revenue streams.
          

          

            Key rating factors: Replacement time for each Critical Item (the key driver) • Number of Critical Items on the critical path • Float/slack in project schedule • Quality of the MWS and contractor • Time deductible (30/45/60 days — larger = lower premium) • Maximum indemnity period
          

        

      

    


    

      
Replacement Time — the critical DSU input

      

        Replacement time = time from re-order to delivery at site (not just manufacturing lead time). Includes: procurement / tendering → manufacturing → factory testing → packing → shipping → site delivery → installation → testing/commissioning. This is what drives the maximum possible delay, and therefore the indemnity period and sum insured.
        


        Post-2020 supply chain disruption has fundamentally changed replacement times. Power transformers: previously 12–18 months, now often 24–36 months. HVDC equipment: 30+ months in some cases. Wind turbine nacelles: 18–24 months. Always verify current lead times with manufacturers — do not rely on historic data or broker estimates. Mid-project, ask the insured to reconfirm lead times annually — if a project started in 2022 with 14-month transformer replacement time, that time has likely changed.
      

    


    

      
Underwriting Best Practice Checklist

      

        
✓ Never write DSU standalone — Section 1 cargo must be confirmed placed
✓ Obtain current (not historic) replacement times from manufacturers
✓ Confirm named MWS before binding — check their track record on similar projects
✓ Time deductible minimum 30 days aggregate; aim 45–60
✓ Maximum indemnity period ≤ longest replacement time + installation/testing period

        
✓ For BI basis (Option A): stress-test the Gross Profit calculation against downside revenue scenarios — would the project have been profitable at all?
✓ For debt service (Option B): review loan agreement — what rate, what maturity, is there a standby credit facility?
✓ Confirm CAR/EAR interface: is there a 50/50 claims clause? Which policy responds first?
✓ Annual replacement time review for multi-year projects
✓ Ensure SCOD revision notification obligation is clearly explained to client at inception

Data Export & Import

        
Back up your data or restore from a previous export

      

      
    


    


      
      

        
📖 How this works

        

          Most legacy workstation data in this build still sits in your browser's localStorage on this device. Email ingest and AI processing now route through the backend.


          Export saves everything to a .json file on your computer. Keep this file somewhere safe (Desktop, Google Drive, email to yourself).


          Import reads a previously exported file and restores your data into this browser. Use this if you switch devices, clear your browser, or need to recover from a bad deploy.
        

      


      
      

        
🔄 Starting a new build session with Claude

        

          When starting a new chat with Claude to continue building this tool:

          
    
            
  1. Download the current HTML file from Netlify (or keep a local copy after each deploy)
    2. 
            
  2. Upload that og-tool-v11.html file to Claude at the start of the conversation
    3. 
            
  3. Your browser data persists automatically — as long as the localStorage key stays the same (og_state_v4), a new deploy won't touch it
    4. 
            
  4. Export your data regularly as a backup in case anything goes wrong
    5.
